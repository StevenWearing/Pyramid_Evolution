The structural evolution of Egyptian Pyramids

From Sand to Skyscraper

There exists a common and likely erroneous belief that the Ancient Egyptians set out to build their great pyramids due to some kind of innate preference for that particular shape. There is, however, quite a wealth of evidence for a logical and often intuitive architectual progression relating to funerary building practices, culminating in the classic pyramid design.

By following along this page's timeframe, beginning at the dawn of Egyptian civilisation and ending halfway through the 4th ruling dynasty's reign, hopefully you can see why most historians view the pyramid's postulated function as an energy source, or anything in such similar vein, as a grandiose overcomplication. And if they are to simply be the result of whimsical experimentation, often fuelled by the aversion of theft, then that does little to take away the sense of awe and wonderment they perpetuate.

SAND BURIAL

In the time of Prehistorical Egypt, before wrtitten records existed, people were layed to rest in makeshift oval burial pits. They likely already had a belief in ressurection, as evidenced by the inclusion of a clay pot within each grave thought to contain food for the after-life.

The close contact with the dry & hot desert provided them with incidental means of natural mummification. But these pits were often swept away by the everchanging landscape of the desert, and this lack of permanancy would have to be addressed.

COFFIN BURIAL

Entering the following Pre-dynastic period, before the pharoahs, these pits took on a rectangular shape and attempts were made to impove their integrity. The dead were placed within terracota or wicker coffins, then the pit's sides were lined with mud-bricks and enclosed beneath a wooden roof. Contact with the sand continued the benefit of natural mummification.

Divisions of society began to surface later on in this period. Class heirarchy displayed itself in the abundances of precious wealth found in a smaller percentage of burials, and males were oft buried alongside their weapons wheras women alongside their cosmetics.

MASTABA BURIAL

To address the problem of theft a larger, more complex structure was needed. The solution arrived around the time of the first dynasty in the form of a Mastaba, a work-bench-like assembly of mud bricks that contained several rooms containing treasures along with an underground burial chamber for the deceased.

Unexpectedly, by moving the body out of contact with the dry desert the natural mummification process was forfeited. And so began the development of artificial means for preservation.

From this point on, Mastaba Burials were the tomb of choice for the upper-class up until around a couple of millenia later, often situated close to their accompanying pharoahs tombs.

STEP PYRAMID

Although this section's pyramid was built for the Pharoah Djoser, it was likely birthed through the mind of his Vizier Imhotep, whom later on would be deified as a god.

Choosing to build at the Saqqara necropolis, a graveyard of sorts, Imhotep took the concept of placing a mastaba atop the grave a few steps further with some experimentation by ultimatley placing 5 more mastabas, each decreasing in size, upon one another. The result was a six-stepped pyramid that was originally plated in white limestone.

SNEFRU'S PYRAMIDS

The first Pharoah of the 4th dynasty, Snefru, was so determined to imporove upon Imhoptep's design that he ended up building 3 monumental structures.

His first attempt was located at Meidum, and still followed the precedent of a step sided pyramid. But, largely due to the lower layers being built upon sand, a large portion this structure eventually sunk beneath the desert.

For his second attempt, Snefru instigated a new necropolis at Dahshur. Advancing yet another step toward the 'true' pyramid shape with the aid of a seamless limestone filling, this pyramid was the first smooth sided. However it initially had too vertical an inclination and so halfway up had to be made less steep. This resulted in a peculiar 'bending' shape.

Finally the pharoah succeeded with his third attempt also located at Dahshur, known as the Red Pyramid, named for the rusty reddish hue of its red limestone stones. inclined at the same angle as the upper half of the bent pyrmaid, this attempt achieved the first 'true' pyramid structure that Snefru had spent most of his life striving to create.

THE GREAT PYRAMID

Following on from his Father's example, Pharoah Khufu would go on to set the standard with his legendary Great Pyramid of Giza. Standing at over 140 meters high, this now rugged structure was once plated in white limestone and adonred with a golden tip that would have reflected large swathes of sunlight over all who gazed upon it for miles around.

Interestingly, what at first glance appears to be the sum of several quite architecturally complex feats is actually thought to be based upon some very basic design principles; for example, its base is almost completely level, but by using a plumb line and bob accomplishing this aspect is made a much more feasible task.
